Generally, if there is something I need to know or a skill I need to learn, I'll do whatever I need to learn it.

    My Relationship With Technology

    One of my biggest focuses in life since I was little has been technology. From phones to computers, hardware to software, 3D printing to embedded electronics, I love all of it. One of my defining qualities is my curiosity, and my ability to rapidly learn new things and adapt that knowledge. I am never quite satisfied with what I know and I'm always trying to find something new to learn about. I feel as though a lot of people fancy themselves a "Jack of all trades" sort of person, I am certainly no exception, but with the wide variety of projects I've worked on (both professionally and personally) I'd like to think that is a claim that I can back up.

    One thing that I've noticed over the years, be it with friends in the IT industry or co-workers, is that work will often make you good at a small handful of specific tasks or applications, but in my personal opinion that rarely makes someone a truly masterful IT professional. For someone to really bring their skills to the next level, it is an absolute requirement that you have a true passion for IT. You should go out of your way to try new things in your free time because you want to, not because you have to. For myself, there is no better way to do this than with my homelab (you should really check it out). Kubernetes, Active Directory, networking, I do a little bit (or sometimes a lot) of everything in my homelab. It's even where I run this website!

    I find that I like to bounce between things a lot. I'll get really into something for a few months, set it up in my homelab, and then promptly forget about it until something breaks. HomeAssistant was like that for me, I set everything up juuust right, and then promptly forgot about it until something would inevitably break. When I started working for Empire Life in 2021, I had exactly zero experience with Kubernetes. I mean, I had heard of it and I knew what it was for, but I had never actually used it. Fast forward to today and it continues to be one of my most-used tools. Every day at work I am working in Kubernetes in some capacity or another, and at home, Kubernetes is the primary driver for my homelab (this website even runs in it). I think the difference with Kubernetes is that there really isn't any end to the number of useful things you can learn about it. It's an infinitely expandable platform (both in terms of scalability and functionality you can add to it) and that is something that I continue to love about it.

    3D printing is pretty neat. It taught me the basics of CAD and has come in handy a few times when I've needed small plastic parts that I would never be able to find in a store or online. It's also fun to print up some more decorative pieces to add a little bit of flair to a room.

    I'm pretty good a cooking, I can make some pretty amazing casseroles and I've got this creamy chicken recipe that I just can't get enough of. During the summer I get into smoking meats, nothing beats fresh smoked fall-off-the-bone ribs!

    One of my stranger hobbies is budgeting. I religiously track my income and spending habits (shoutout to YNAB) and use that information to try an extrapolate a forecast for my wealth-building. Believe it or not, budgeting is something I really enjoy (seeing money leave my account, not so much). Money in general is something that really interests me, macroeconomic trends are something that I spend a lot of time reading about and trying to understand as well.

    Finally, there is my dog Ruby. I love spending time with her. Walking her, training her, and just hanging out with her. She is one of the biggest highlights in my life.
